## 2. Irrigation Methods

### 2A. Flood Irrigation (Traditional / Surface Irrigation)

**How it works:** Water is released from a channel or pipe at the top end of a field and allowed to flow across the entire field by gravity. The field is divided into basins, borders, or checks using small bunds. Water spreads uniformly and infiltrates into the soil while flowing across the surface.

**Best for:** Rice (paddy), wheat (in parts of Punjab/Haryana), sugarcane (in Maharashtra/UP). Most widely used method in India â€” ~78% of irrigated area still uses surface methods.

**Water efficiency:** 30-40% (only 30-40% of applied water is actually used by the plant; 60-70% is lost to deep percolation, evaporation, and runoff).

**Pros:**
- Low initial investment â€” no equipment needed
- Simple to operate â€” no technical skill required
- Suitable for heavy clay soils with low infiltration rates
- Works well for crops that need standing water (paddy)

**Cons:**
- Extremely water-wasteful (highest water consumption of all methods)
- Leads to waterlogging, salinity, and nutrient leaching
- Uneven water distribution across field (head vs tail end)
- Promotes weed growth and certain fungal diseases
- Labour intensive â€” requires constant field supervision

**Water Loss Breakdown:**
| Loss Type | Percentage |
|---|---|
| Deep percolation | 25-35% |
| Evaporation from soil surface | 10-15% |
| Conveyance losses (canal seepage) | 15-25% |
| Runoff | 5-10% |
| Effective water reaching roots | 30-40% |

### 2B. Drip Irrigation (Micro-Irrigation)

**How it works:** Water is delivered directly to the root zone of each plant through a network of pipes, valves, tubing, and emitters (drippers). Water drips slowly at a rate of 2-20 litres/hour per emitter, maintaining optimal soil moisture. The system operates at low pressure (0.5-1.5 kg/cmÂ²). Components include: mainline, sub-mainline, laterals (16mm LLDPE pipes), inline/online drippers, filters (screen/disc/sand), and fertigation unit.

**Best for:** Cotton, sugarcane, banana, mango, coconut, pomegranate, grapes, citrus, tomato, chilli, onion, capsicum, all orchard crops, greenhouse crops. Ideal for wide-spaced crops, row crops, and horticultural crops.

**Water efficiency:** 85-95% (highest among all irrigation methods).

### 2C. Sprinkler Irrigation

**How it works:** Water is sprayed into the air through nozzles under pressure (2-4 kg/cmÂ²), breaking into small droplets that fall on the crop like artificial rainfall. System types include: (a) Portable hand-move sprinklers, (b) Semi-permanent sprinklers, (c) Permanent solid-set sprinklers, (d) Centre pivot systems, (e) Rain gun sprinklers (for large area coverage up to 50m radius).

**Best for:** Wheat, groundnut, pulses, maize, millets, fodder crops, soybean, mustard, potato, vegetables (except tomato during fruiting). Ideal for closely spaced field crops, sandy soils, and undulating terrain.

**Water efficiency:** 65-80% (medium-high).

- Salt burn on foliage if water has high salinity

### 2D. Furrow Irrigation

**How it works:** Water flows along small channels (furrows) dug between crop rows. Furrows are typically 10-30 cm deep and 30-60 cm wide, with spacing matching crop row spacing. Water infiltrates the soil from the sides and bottom of the furrows. Types: (a) Straight furrows (flat land), (b) Contour furrows (sloped land), (c) Corrugation furrows (small, closely spaced), (d) Surge irrigation (alternating furrow flow for better uniformity).

**Best for:** Sugarcane (60-90 cm furrows), cotton, maize, potato, vegetables (tomato, brinjal, chilli, cabbage), all row crops. Very popular in Maharashtra, Gujarat, UP for sugarcane.

**Water efficiency:** 45-60% (better than flood, worse than drip/sprinkler).

**Pros:**
- Lower cost than drip/sprinkler â€” needs only land shaping
- Keeps plant foliage dry (reduces disease)
- Allows tractor/bullock movement between rows
- Good for heavy clay soils
- Compatible with mechanical cultivation

**Cons:**
- Uneven water distribution (head end gets more than tail end)
- Requires well-levelled land
- Significant deep percolation losses
- Labour intensive for furrow maintenance
- Not suitable for closely spaced crops

**Advanced Technique â€” Alternate Furrow Irrigation (AFI):**
- Water only every alternate furrow (skip one, irrigate one)
- Saves 25-35% water with only 5-10% yield reduction
- Proven effective in sugarcane (Maharashtra), cotton (Gujarat)
- ICAR recommends AFI for water-scarce regions

### 2E. System of Rice Intensification (SRI)

**How it works:** SRI is a methodology for increasing rice productivity by modifying plant, soil, water, and nutrient management. Key principles: (1) Transplant young seedlings (8-12 days old, 2-leaf stage) instead of 21-25 days old, (2) Plant single seedlings with wider spacing (25cm Ã— 25cm), (3) Alternate Wetting and Drying (AWD) â€” maintain thin layer of water or moist soil instead of continuous flooding, (4) Use mechanical weeder for soil aeration, (5) Apply organic manure along with recommended fertilizers. Fields are kept just moist during vegetative phase and thin water layer (2-3 cm) maintained only during flowering.

**Water savings:** 25-50% reduction compared to conventional flood irrigation. Typical water use: 700-900 mm per crop cycle vs 1200-2000 mm for conventional paddy.

**Yield impact:** 20-50% higher yields reported. National average yield increases from 3.5 t/ha to 5-7 t/ha with SRI. Root development improves by 50-80%, tillering increases by 100-200%.

**Adoption in India:**
- **Widely adopted:** Tamil Nadu (60,000+ ha), Andhra Pradesh (1,00,000+ ha under APCNF), Tripura (leading NE state), Odisha, Bihar, Jharkhand
- **State programs:** Tamil Nadu â€” SRI promoted through TNAU; Andhra Pradesh â€” Community Managed Natural Farming integrates SRI; Tripura â€” state-wide SRI mission
- **Challenges:** Labour-intensive transplanting, weed management requires mechanical weeder, not suitable for all soil types (very sandy or very heavy clay)

### 4C. Alternate Wetting and Drying (AWD) for Rice â€” Detailed Protocol

**What:** AWD is a water management technique where rice fields are alternately flooded and allowed to dry to a safe level, instead of continuous flooding.

**Step-by-Step AWD Protocol:**
1. **Install AWD tube:** Place a perforated PVC pipe (15 cm diameter, 25 cm long, holes in lower 15 cm) into the soil â€” water level inside tube indicates field water level
2. **After transplanting:** Maintain 3-5 cm standing water for first 15 days (establishment)
3. **During tillering (15-45 DAT):** Allow water level to drop to 15 cm below soil surface (check using AWD tube). When water in tube drops to this level, re-irrigate to 5 cm depth
4. **During panicle initiation to flowering (45-90 DAT):** ðŸ”´ Maintain 3-5 cm standing water continuously â€” NO AWD during this critical period
5. **During grain filling (90-110 DAT):** Resume AWD â€” allow water to drop to 15 cm below surface before re-irrigating
6. **Pre-harvest (10-15 days before):** Drain completely

**AWD Safe Threshold:** Water level should NEVER drop below 15 cm below soil surface. Below this, the crop experiences stress.

### 4D. Deficit Irrigation â€” Strategic Water Stress

**Concept:** Deliberately under-irrigating crops at specific non-critical stages to save water while minimizing yield loss. The goal is to maximize "crop per drop" â€” water productivity, not necessarily maximum yield.

**Crops That Tolerate Deficit Irrigation Well:**

|---|---|---|---|---|
