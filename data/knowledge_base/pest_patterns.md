### A1. Rice Blast

**Scientific Name:** Pyricularia oryzae (Magnaporthe oryzae)
**Crop Affected:** Rice (all varieties, especially high-yield varieties like Pusa Basmati, Swarna)
**Type:** Fungal
**Season/Conditions:** Kharif season, high humidity (>90%), temperature 25-28Â°C, overcast/cloudy weather, excessive nitrogen application, closely spaced planting

**Symptoms:**
- **Leaf blast:** Diamond/spindle-shaped spots with grey centre and brown margin on leaves (0.5-1.5 cm long)
- **Neck blast:** Infection at panicle base â€” neck turns brown/black, panicle breaks or droops, grain filling stops completely
- **Node blast:** Blackish lesions on stem nodes, stem weakens and breaks
- **Early stage:** Small blue-green specks (pin-head size) that enlarge rapidly within 3-5 days

**Economic Impact:** Can cause 50-90% yield loss in severe outbreaks. Neck blast is most destructive â€” entire panicle is lost. India loses an estimated â‚¹7,000-10,000 crore annually to rice blast.

**Organic/Traditional Treatment:**
- Spray Pseudomonas fluorescens @ 10 g/L or 2.5 kg/ha as preventive (2-3 sprays at 10-day intervals)
- Apply Trichoderma viride @ 5 g/L as foliar spray
- Neem oil 5% spray reduces spore germination
- Maintain 20Ã—15 cm spacing â€” avoid dense planting to improve air circulation
- Drain fields periodically to reduce microclimate humidity

**Chemical Treatment:**
- **Preventive:** Tricyclazole 75 WP @ 0.6 g/L (spray at tillering + booting stage)
- **Curative:** Isoprothiolane 40 EC @ 1.5 mL/L (spray at first symptom appearance)
- **Alternative:** Carbendazim 50 WP @ 1 g/L or Edifenphos 50 EC @ 1 mL/L
- **Combination:** Tricyclazole 18% + Mancozeb 62% WP @ 2.5 g/L (broad-spectrum protection)

**Prevention:**
- Use resistant varieties: IR-64, Tetep, Rasi, Co-39, Pusa 1602 (blast-resistant)
- Balanced fertilization â€” apply nitrogen in 3 splits (basal + tillering + panicle initiation), do NOT exceed 120 kg N/ha
- Remove and burn infected stubble after harvest (pathogen survives in debris)
- Treat seeds with Carbendazim 50 WP @ 2 g/kg or Tricyclazole @ 3 g/kg before sowing
- Avoid late planting â€” early Kharif planting escapes peak blast period

### A2. Wheat Rust (Yellow/Brown/Black Rust)

**Scientific Name:** Puccinia striiformis f.sp. tritici (Yellow/Stripe rust), Puccinia triticina (Brown/Leaf rust), Puccinia graminis f.sp. tritici (Black/Stem rust)
**Crop Affected:** Wheat (all varieties, though severity varies)
**Type:** Fungal (Obligate parasite â€” Basidiomycetes)
**Season/Conditions:** Yellow rust appears first in cooler areas (Dec-Jan) at 10-15Â°C with dew/light rain; Brown rust at 15-25Â°C (Jan-Feb) with high humidity; Black rust at 25-30Â°C (Feb-Mar), late season. All need moisture on leaf surface for spore germination.

**Symptoms:**
- **Yellow rust (Stripe rust):** Yellow-orange powdery pustules arranged in linear stripes/rows along leaf veins. Appears first on lower leaves, moves upward. Leaves turn yellow and dry prematurely. Most damaging at ear emergence.
- **Brown rust (Leaf rust):** Small, round to oval, orange-brown pustules scattered randomly on upper leaf surface (not in rows). Leaves look "rusty". Most common rust in India. Pustules 1-2 mm diameter.
- **Black rust (Stem rust):** Large, dark reddish-brown to black pustules on stems, leaf sheaths, and glumes. Pustules rupture epidermis â€” rough to touch. Appears late in season. Can destroy crop within 2-3 weeks if conditions favourable.

**Economic Impact:** Yellow rust can cause 40-100% loss in susceptible varieties. Brown rust causes 10-30% yield reduction (most widespread). Black rust is potentially the most devastating (historically caused famines) but now rare due to resistant varieties. Combined annual loss: â‚¹2,000-5,000 crore in India.

**Organic/Traditional Treatment:**
- Remove alternate host plants (Berberis spp.) near wheat fields
- Apply Pseudomonas fluorescens @ 10 g/L as preventive foliar spray at tillering
- Spray neem oil 2% at 15-day intervals starting from CRI stage
- Early sowing (before Nov 15 in North India) helps crop escape late-season black rust

**Chemical Treatment:**
- **Preventive:** Mancozeb 75 WP @ 2.5 g/L (2 sprays at 15-day intervals from first symptom)
- **Curative (Best option):** Propiconazole 25 EC (Tilt) @ 1 mL/L â€” single spray controls all three rusts for 15-20 days
- **Alternative:** Tebuconazole 25.9 EC @ 1 mL/L
- **Combination:** Trifloxystrobin 25% + Tebuconazole 50% WG (Nativo) @ 0.4 g/L
- **Timing:** First spray at disease appearance, second spray 15 days later. Do NOT spray after grain filling (dough stage).

**Prevention:**
- **Most important:** Grow resistant varieties â€” HD-3226 (Pusa Yashasvi), DBW-187, PBW-826, WH-1270 (check ICAR-IIWBR latest recommendations)
- Early sowing: Complete sowing by Nov 15 (North India) to escape rust window
- Avoid excessive nitrogen â€” promotes lush growth susceptible to rust
- Balanced potassium application improves rust resistance
- Monitor ICAR Rust Surveillance Advisory (issued weekly Dec-Mar) for early warning
- Destroy volunteer wheat and self-sown wheat plants that harbour rust between seasons

> **Verified from:** ICAR-IIWBR Karnal Wheat Disease Guide (https://www.iiwbr.org/), TNAU Agritech Wheat Crop Protection (https://agritech.tnau.ac.in/crop_protection/wheat.html), ICAR Rust Monitoring System

### A3. Cotton Bollworm (American Bollworm)

**Scientific Name:** Helicoverpa armigera (HÃ¼bner)
**Crop Affected:** Cotton (primary). Also severely attacks chickpea (gram), tomato, pigeon pea (toor), maize, sunflower, groundnut, sorghum â€” India's most polyphagous pest.
**Type:** Insect pest â€” Lepidoptera (Noctuidae). Moth lays eggs, larvae (caterpillars) cause damage.
**Season/Conditions:** Active throughout Kharif season (Jul-Nov). Peak damage during cotton boll formation (Aug-Oct). Moths active at dusk. Thrives in warm (25-35Â°C), humid conditions. Outbreaks worse in years with intermittent dry spells during monsoon.

**Symptoms:**
- **On cotton:** Young larvae feed on squares (flower buds) and flowers. Older larvae bore into bolls â€” entry hole with frass (excreta) visible outside. Boll rots from inside, opens prematurely, lint stained and unmarketable. One larva can destroy 20-30 bolls in its lifetime.
- **On tomato:** Larvae bore into green/ripe fruits. Circular entry hole, fruit rots. Multiple fruits on same plant attacked.
- **On chickpea:** Larvae feed inside pods, consuming developing grains. Green frass pellets visible on plant.
- **Egg identification:** Round, yellowish-white eggs (0.5 mm) laid singly on tender plant parts (terminals, flowers, young bolls).
- **Larva identification:** Green to brown caterpillar, 30-40 mm when mature, with dark lateral stripes and scattered dark spots. Nocturnal feeders.

**Economic Impact:** India's costliest crop pest â€” annual losses estimated at â‚¹5,000-10,000 crore across all host crops. Can destroy 50-80% of cotton bolls in severe outbreaks. Before Bt cotton, farmers spent â‚¹2,000-4,000/acre on bollworm insecticides alone.

**Organic/Traditional Treatment:**
- **Pheromone traps:** Install Helicoverpa pheromone traps @ 5 traps/acre at flowering stage. Monitor moth catches â€” spray when >8 moths/trap/night
- **Trichogramma chilonis:** Release egg parasitoid @ 1.5 lakh eggs/ha (6-8 releases at weekly intervals from squaring stage). Available from KVKs and biocontrol labs
- **HaNPV (Nuclear Polyhedrosis Virus):** Spray @ 250 LE (Larval Equivalents)/ha at first larva sighting. Apply in evening (UV-sensitive). Mix with 0.1% jaggery solution as feeding stimulant
- **Neem seed kernel extract (NSKE):** 5% spray (50 g neem seed kernel powder in 1 litre water, filter and spray) â€” acts as antifeedant and growth regulator
- **Bird perches:** Erect T-shaped bird perches @ 50/ha â€” attracts insectivorous birds (shrikes, drongos)
- **Intercropping:** Grow trap crops â€” pigeon pea, marigold around cotton field margins. Helicoverpa lays eggs on trap crop, reduces cotton damage

**Chemical Treatment:**
- **Threshold for spraying:** >5% damage to squares or >10% damaged bolls, OR 1 larva/plant
- **First choice:** Emamectin benzoate 5 SG @ 0.4 g/L (very effective against all larval stages)
- **Alternative 1:** Chlorantraniliprole 18.5 SC (Coragen) @ 0.3 mL/L â€” safe for natural enemies
- **Alternative 2:** Spinosad 45 SC @ 0.3 mL/L (derived from soil bacterium, IPM-compatible)
- **Alternative 3:** Indoxacarb 14.5 SC @ 0.5 mL/L
- **AVOID:** Synthetic pyrethroids (cypermethrin, deltamethrin) â€” Helicoverpa has developed high resistance in most Indian regions. Avoid organophosphates repeatedly.
- **Rotation:** Rotate chemical groups in each spray to prevent resistance. Never use same insecticide group more than 2 times in a season.

**Prevention:**
- **Bt cotton:** Grow approved Bt cotton hybrids (BG-II) containing Cry1Ac + Cry2Ab genes â€” provides 80-90% bollworm protection. Plant 20% non-Bt refuge (5 rows around Bt field) as per government mandate
- Deep summer ploughing to expose pupae to sun and predators
- Remove and destroy crop residues after harvest â€” pupae survive in soil
- Avoid continuous cotton monoculture â€” rotate with non-host crops (wheat, millets, groundnut)
- Early sowing (before June 15 in Central India) helps crop escape peak moth flight
- Install light traps @ 1/5 acres to monitor adult moth activity

> **Verified from:** ICAR-CICR Nagpur Cotton IPM Guide (https://www.cicr.org.in/), NCIPM IPM Package for Cotton (http://ncipm.org.in/), TNAU Agritech Cotton Crop Protection (https://agritech.tnau.ac.in/crop_protection/cotton.html)

### A4. Late Blight of Potato & Tomato

**Scientific Name:** Phytophthora infestans (Mont.) de Bary
**Crop Affected:** Potato (primary host), Tomato (major host). Also affects brinjal, chilli, capsicum, and other Solanaceae family crops.
**Type:** Oomycete (water mould) â€” often grouped with fungi but technically a separate kingdom. Spreads through airborne sporangia.
**Season/Conditions:** Cool, wet weather â€” optimal at 15-22Â°C with relative humidity >90% and prolonged leaf wetness (>10 hours). In India, severe during Rabi season (Nov-Feb) in Indo-Gangetic plains, and during monsoon (Jul-Sep) in hills and South India. Fog and drizzle create ideal infection conditions.

**Symptoms:**
- **Potato leaves:** Irregular, water-soaked, pale green to dark brown lesions starting from leaf tips and margins. White cottony fungal growth (sporangia) visible on underside of leaves in early morning. Leaves blacken and die rapidly â€” entire foliage can be destroyed in 7-10 days ("blighting").
- **Potato tubers:** Reddish-brown, irregular, shallow depressions on skin. Internal tissue shows rusty-brown dry rot extending inward from surface. Infected tubers rot in storage, often with secondary bacterial soft rot.
- **Tomato:** Dark brown water-soaked lesions on leaves, stems, and green fruits. Fruits develop dark, firm, greasy-looking spots. White sporulation on fruit surface in humid conditions.
- **Disease spread:** Extremely fast â€” one infected plant can spread disease to entire field in 5-7 days under favourable conditions. Sporangia travel by wind up to 10-15 km.

**Economic Impact:** Historically the most devastating crop disease (caused Irish Potato Famine, 1845). In India, causes 20-75% potato yield loss in epidemic years. Annual loss in potato alone estimated at â‚¹3,000-5,000 crore. Tomato losses of 30-60% common in hill regions.

**Organic/Traditional Treatment:**
- Spray Trichoderma viride @ 5 g/L as preventive from 30 days after planting, repeat every 10 days
- Bordeaux mixture (1% â€” prepared from 1 kg CuSO4 + 1 kg lime + 100 L water) â€” oldest effective treatment, still widely used. Spray every 7-10 days in disease-prone period
- Good drainage â€” avoid waterlogging. Ridge planting reduces tuber infection
- Remove and destroy first infected plants immediately (rogue out) to slow spread

**Chemical Treatment:**
- **Preventive (before disease appears):** Mancozeb 75 WP @ 2.5 g/L â€” spray starting 30-35 days after planting, repeat every 7-10 days during cool-wet weather
- **Curative (after disease appears):** Metalaxyl 8% + Mancozeb 64% WP (Ridomil Gold) @ 2.5 g/L â€” systemic + contact action
- **Alternative:** Cymoxanil 8% + Mancozeb 64% WP (Curzate M8) @ 3 g/L
- **Advanced option:** Dimethomorph 50 WP @ 1 g/L or Fenamidone 10% + Mancozeb 50% WDG @ 3 g/L
- **Spray schedule:** Start preventive sprays at 30-35 DAP. If disease appears, switch to curative fungicide. Alternate contact + systemic fungicides to prevent resistance. Total 5-7 sprays per season.

**Prevention:**
- Use certified disease-free seed tubers (from cold storage, treated with Mancozeb 0.25% dip)
- Grow resistant varieties: Kufri Jyoti, Kufri Badshah, Kufri Girdhari (potato); Arka Rakshak, Arka Samrat (tomato â€” contain Ph-2 and Ph-3 resistance genes)
- Avoid overhead irrigation â€” use furrow or drip to keep foliage dry
- Adequate spacing for air circulation (60Ã—20 cm for potato)
- Hilling up (earthing up) â€” covers tubers, prevents sporangia from reaching tubers through soil
- Dehaulming â€” cut and remove foliage 10-15 days before harvest to prevent tuber infection
- Use ICAR-CPRI late blight forecast model (www.cpri.org.in) â€” spray schedule based on weather prediction

> **Verified from:** ICAR-CPRI Shimla Late Blight Management (https://cpri.icar.gov.in/), TNAU Agritech Potato Diseases (https://agritech.tnau.ac.in/crop_protection/potato.html), NCIPM IPM Package for Potato (http://ncipm.org.in/)

### A5. Bacterial Leaf Blight (BLB) of Rice

**Scientific Name:** Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae (Xoo)
**Crop Affected:** Rice (all varieties, especially susceptible during Kharif)
**Type:** Bacterial
**Season/Conditions:** High temperature (25-34Â°C), high humidity (>80%), heavy rainfall, strong winds (spread bacteria through wounds), excessive nitrogen fertilization, clipping of leaf tips during transplanting. Most severe during tillering to flowering stage.

**Symptoms:**
- **Kresek (seedling wilt):** Seedlings in nursery wilt and die within 2-3 weeks of transplanting. Leaves turn pale yellow and roll. Entire plant dries up. Occurs when infected seedlings are transplanted.
- **Leaf blight phase:** Water-soaked lesions start from leaf tip or margins, turning yellow to white and drying along leaf veins. Lesions extend downward and inward. Affected leaves dry completely, turning straw-coloured.
- **Bacterial ooze:** In early morning (with dew), yellowish bacterial ooze (milky droplets) visible at leaf edges â€” confirmatory diagnostic sign.
- **Entire field view:** Field looks scorched â€” white-yellow dried leaves visible from distance. Grain filling severely affected.

**Economic Impact:** Yield loss of 20-50% in moderate infection, up to 80% in severe Kresek phase. Widespread in West Bengal, Odisha, eastern UP, Bihar, AP, and all high-rainfall rice areas.

**Organic/Traditional Treatment:**
- Spray Pseudomonas fluorescens @ 10 g/L at 15-day intervals starting from tillering (proven effective in reducing BLB severity by 40-50%)
- Apply fresh cowdung extract (20%) as foliar spray â€” traditional practice in eastern India, provides some suppression
- Drain excess water from fields â€” standing water promotes bacterial spread
- Avoid clipping leaf tips during transplanting (creates entry wounds for bacteria)

**Chemical Treatment:**
- **Note:** Antibiotics and chemical fungicides have LIMITED effect on BLB. Copper-based compounds are the best chemical option.
- **Copper oxychloride 50 WP @ 3 g/L** â€” spray at first symptom, repeat after 10 days
- **Streptomycin sulphate 0.01% (100 ppm)** + Copper oxychloride 0.05% â€” combination spray
- **Copper hydroxide 77 WP @ 2.5 g/L** â€” alternative copper formulation
- **Avoid:** Regular fungicides (Carbendazim, Mancozeb) are NOT effective against BLB â€” it's bacterial, not fungal

**Prevention:**
- **Resistant varieties (BEST strategy):** Improved Samba Mahsuri (ISM), Pusa 1460, Rajendra Dhan-201, CR Dhan-310 (carry Xa21, xa13, xa5 resistance genes)
- Do NOT clip seedling leaf tips during transplanting
- Balanced NPK â€” reduce nitrogen to 80-100 kg/ha in BLB-prone areas (excess N increases susceptibility dramatically)
- Apply potassium (Kâ‚‚O @ 60 kg/ha) â€” potassium strengthens cell walls against bacterial entry
- Avoid flood irrigation immediately after strong winds/storms (wounds + bacteria + water = rapid spread)
- Seed treatment with Streptocycline (0.01%) for 30 minutes before sowing

> **Verified from:** ICAR-CRRI Rice BLB Management (https://icar-crri.org/), TNAU Agritech Rice Bacterial Diseases (https://agritech.tnau.ac.in/crop_protection/rice/rice_1.html), IRRI Knowledge Bank (https://www.irri.org/)

### A6. Downy Mildew of Millets (Pearl Millet / Bajra)

**Scientific Name:** Sclerospora graminicola (Sacc.) Schroet.
**Crop Affected:** Pearl millet (Bajra) â€” primary host. Also affects foxtail millet, sorghum (different Sclerospora species).
**Type:** Oomycete (downy mildew pathogen)
**Season/Conditions:** Kharif season. Cool nights (20-25Â°C) followed by warm days (30-35Â°C), high soil moisture at seedling stage, relative humidity >85%. Sandy and sandy-loam soils with poor drainage. Pathogen survives as oospores in soil for 5-10 years.

**Symptoms:**
- **Seedling stage:** Chlorosis (yellowing) of leaves starting from base. Leaves show half-leaf chlorosis (green-yellow boundary along midrib). White cottony fungal growth on underside of leaves (visible in early morning).
- **"Green ear" / "crazy top":** Most characteristic symptom â€” panicle (ear head) is transformed into a green leafy structure instead of grain-bearing ear. Florets turn into leaf-like growths. Severely infected plants produce no grain at all.
- **Systemic infection:** Entire plant is stunted with excessive tillering (witch's broom appearance). Leaves thick, leathery, light green to yellow. Plant remains vegetative â€” does not flower normally.

**Economic Impact:** Major constraint to bajra production in Rajasthan, Gujarat, Haryana, Maharashtra. Can cause 30-70% yield loss in susceptible hybrids. Severe epidemics occurred in 1970s when first single-cross hybrids (HB-3) were introduced without DM resistance.

**Organic/Traditional Treatment:**
- Apply Trichoderma viride @ 4 g/kg seed as seed treatment
- Mix FYM (Farm Yard Manure) with Trichoderma (1 kg Trichoderma in 100 kg FYM, incubate 7 days) â€” apply to soil before sowing
- Remove and destroy infected plants (roguing) as soon as green ear symptoms appear â€” prevents oospore buildup in soil
- Crop rotation with non-host crops (legumes, oilseeds) for 3 years to reduce soil oospore load

**Chemical Treatment:**
- **Seed treatment (MOST effective):** Metalaxyl 35 SD (Apron) @ 6 g/kg seed â€” provides systemic protection for 30-40 days. This single treatment prevents 80-90% of downy mildew.
- **Alternative seed treatment:** Metalaxyl 2 g + Thiram 3 g per kg seed
- **Foliar spray (if symptoms appear):** Metalaxyl 8% + Mancozeb 64% WP (Ridomil Gold) @ 2.5 g/L â€” spray at 20-25 DAS
- **Soil application:** Metalaxyl granules @ 1 kg a.i./ha mixed with sand, broadcast at sowing in severely infested fields

**Prevention:**
- **Resistant hybrids (CRITICAL):** Grow ICRISAT/ICAR-released DM-resistant hybrids â€” HHB-67 Improved, GHB-558, RHB-173, RHB-177, MPMH-17. Check state agricultural university recommendation for your region
- Seed treatment with Metalaxyl is mandatory in DM-endemic areas
- Avoid planting same hybrid in the same field for more than 3 consecutive years (pathogen overcomes resistance)
- Deep ploughing in summer â€” exposes soil oospores to sunlight and desiccation
- Avoid waterlogging at seedling stage

> **Verified from:** ICRISAT Pearl Millet Pathology (https://www.icrisat.org/), ICAR-AICPMIP Downy Mildew Management, TNAU Agritech Millets Diseases (https://agritech.tnau.ac.in/)

### A7. Panama Wilt of Banana (Fusarium Wilt)

**Scientific Name:** Fusarium oxysporum f.sp. cubense (Foc) â€” Race 1 (affects Rasthali, Monthan, Silk varieties) and Tropical Race 4 / TR4 (threatens Cavendish/Grand Naine â€” GLOBAL EMERGENCY)
**Crop Affected:** Banana â€” all varieties susceptible to one or more races
**Type:** Fungal (Soil-borne â€” Fusarium)
**Season/Conditions:** Warm temperatures (25-30Â°C), acidic soils (pH < 6.0), waterlogged or poorly drained soils, mechanical root damage (during weeding/ploughing), presence of root-knot nematodes. Fungus survives in soil for 20-30+ years as chlamydospores.

**Symptoms:**
- **External:** Yellowing of oldest (lower) leaves starting from leaf margins, progressing inward. Yellowed leaves droop and hang around pseudostem like a "skirt". Pseudostem may split at base. Plant wilts and collapses. Typically affects plants at 5-7 months (before or during fruiting).
- **Internal (confirmatory):** Cut the pseudostem cross-wise â€” dark brown to reddish-brown discoloration of vascular bundles visible (ring of brown dots/lines in cross-section). This vascular browning is the hallmark diagnostic sign.
- **Root system:** Brown, rotted roots. Rhizome shows brown vascular streaks.
- **Progression:** Disease is one-sided initially â€” only some leaf sheaths show browning. Eventually entire plant dies. Bunches if present are small with thin, unmarketable fingers.

**Economic Impact:** One of the most devastating banana diseases globally. Destroyed Gros Michel banana industry worldwide in the 1950s. In India, Race 1 causes significant losses in traditional varieties (Rasthali in TN, Monthan in Kerala). **TR4 is the biggest threat** â€” first reported in India in Bihar (2017) and has potential to devastate the â‚¹50,000 crore Indian banana industry.

**Organic/Traditional Treatment:**
- Apply Trichoderma viride (or T. harzianum) @ 50 g/plant in planting pit â€” repeat at 2-month intervals (colonizes root zone, suppresses Fusarium)
- Apply Pseudomonas fluorescens @ 20 g/plant in planting pit
- Drench soil with Carbendazim 50 WP @ 2 g/L as a bio-chemical approach
- Apply neem cake @ 2 kg/planting pit (releases azadirachtin which suppresses soil fungi)
- Maintain soil pH at 6.5-7.0 by liming acidic soils (Fusarium thrives in acidic soils)
- Avoid flood irrigation â€” use drip to prevent waterlogging and root damage

**Chemical Treatment:**
- **Note:** No chemical treatment can CURE Panama wilt once plant is infected â€” the pathogen is inside vascular system. Focus is on soil treatment and prevention.
- **Soil drenching (preventive):** Carbendazim 50 WP @ 2 g/L â€” drench 10 litres around plant base at 3-month intervals
- **Planting pit treatment:** Carbendazim 25 g + Trichoderma 50 g per pit at planting
- **Infected plant management:** Inject affected plant with 2% Carbendazim (50 mL into pseudostem) â€” may save early-infected plants. Destroy severely infected plants completely (uproot, chop, burn or bury with quicklime)

**Prevention:**
- **Resistant varieties (MOST important):** Grand Naine (Cavendish) is resistant to Race 1 (but susceptible to TR4). For Race 1 areas: grow Grand Naine, Robusta, NRCB selection. For TR4: no fully resistant commercial variety yet â€” ICAR-NRCB is developing resistant lines
- Use tissue culture plantlets from certified labs (disease-free planting material)
- Strict quarantine â€” do NOT bring suckers from infected areas
- Apply lime @ 2 kg/pit if soil pH < 6.0
- Practice crop rotation â€” grow paddy (flooding kills Fusarium) for 2-3 years before replanting banana
- Avoid injuring roots during cultivation â€” Fusarium enters through wounds
- **TR4 containment:** If TR4 suspected â€” report immediately to state agriculture department. Quarantine zone, destroy infected plants, disinfect tools with 2% formalin. Do NOT move soil/suckers from affected area.

> **Verified from:** ICAR-NRCB Tiruchirappalli Panama Wilt Management (https://nrcb.icar.gov.in/), TNAU Agritech Banana Diseases (https://agritech.tnau.ac.in/crop_protection/banana.html), FAO Fusarium Wilt TR4 Global Alert (https://www.fao.org/)

### A8. Bunchy Top of Banana (BBTV)

**Scientific Name:** Banana Bunchy Top Virus (BBTV) â€” Family Nanoviridae
**Crop Affected:** Banana (all varieties susceptible, Cavendish/Grand Naine most affected)
**Type:** Viral disease, transmitted by banana aphid (Pentalonia nigronervosa)
**Season/Conditions:** Year-round in tropical conditions. Aphid populations peak in warm, humid weather (25-30Â°C, >80% RH). Spread primarily through infected suckers (planting material) and aphid vectors. NOT transmitted through soil or mechanically.

**Symptoms:**
- **Leaf symptoms:** New leaves are narrow, shortened, with upturned/rolled margins. Leaves become stiff, brittle, and erect (bunched together at the top â€” hence "bunchy top"). Dark green streaks/dots ("Morse code" pattern or "J-hook" lines) along leaf veins and midrib â€” visible when held against light.
- **Plant growth:** Severely stunted plant. Internodes shortened â€” leaves appear crowded at apex. Plant remains dwarfed (rosetting).
- **Fruiting:** Infected plants rarely produce bunches. If bunches form, they are small with distorted, thin fingers. No economic yield.
- **Progression:** Once infected, the plant never recovers. Virus is systemic â€” spreads to suckers and ratoon crops.

**Economic Impact:** Most serious viral disease of banana in India. Can cause 100% loss in severely infected plantations. Major problem in Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Assam, Bihar. An estimated 10-20% of Indian banana plantations affected to some degree.

**Organic/Traditional Treatment:**
- **No cure exists for viral diseases.** Infected plants must be completely destroyed.
- Spray neem oil 2% or azadirachtin-based formulations to repel aphid vectors
- Release ladybird beetles (Coccinellids) â€” natural predators of aphids
- Apply sticky yellow traps around field periphery to trap migrating aphids
- Maintain clean field borders â€” remove weeds that harbour aphids

**Chemical Treatment (Vector Control):**
- **Aphid control is the only chemical management strategy:**
- Imidacloprid 17.8 SL @ 0.5 mL/L â€” systemic spray against aphids
- Dimethoate 30 EC @ 1.7 mL/L â€” spray on sucker/plant base where aphids colonize
- Thiamethoxam 25 WG @ 0.5 g/L â€” neonicotinoid for aphid control
- **Sucker dipping:** Dip suckers in Chlorpyrifos 20 EC @ 5 mL/L for 30 minutes before planting (kills aphids on planting material)
- **Inject:** Inject pseudostem of infected plants with Glyphosate 10 mL/plant before uprooting to kill plant and prevent regrowth

**Prevention:**
- **Virus-free planting material (MOST critical):** Use ONLY tissue culture plants from ICAR/State Government accredited labs. NEVER use suckers from infected areas.
- **Rogue immediately:** Uproot and destroy infected plants as soon as symptoms appear (within 7 days). Inject with herbicide, chop, and bury deep or burn.
- Spray aphicide (Imidacloprid) in 3m radius around infected plant before destroying it â€” kills aphid vectors that carry virus
- Maintain 3-year virus-free source nurseries
- Quarantine: Do NOT transport planting material from BBTV-endemic areas to clean zones
- Monitor field fortnightly â€” early detection + immediate destruction is the ONLY way to contain spread

### B7. Red Palm Weevil â€” Coconut, Oil Palm, Date Palm

**Scientific Name:** Rhynchophorus ferrugineus (Olivier)
**Crop Affected:** Coconut (primary in India), Oil palm, Date palm, Arecanut, Sago palm
**Type:** Insect pest â€” Coleoptera (Curculionidae). Grub (larva) bores into trunk and crown, feeds internally.
**Season/Conditions:** Active year-round in tropical India. Adults fly and colonize palms mainly during monsoon (Jun-Sep) and post-monsoon. Female lays 200-300 eggs in wounds/cut surfaces on trunk. Larval development inside trunk takes 2-3 months.

**Symptoms:**
- **Early signs:** Small holes (1-2 cm) on trunk with brown fibrous frass oozing out. Chewed fibre material near crown base.
- **Crown symptoms:** Central unopened leaves (spindle) wilt, turn yellow, and droop. Crown becomes lopsided as inner tissues are destroyed.
- **Advanced stage:** Central crown collapses ("toppling of crown"). Fermented toddy-like smell from trunk. Gnawing sound audible if ear placed on trunk.
- **Fatal:** By the time external symptoms are clearly visible, 60-70% internal damage is done. Adult palm can die within 6-12 months of infestation.

**Economic Impact:** Most destructive pest of coconut worldwide. Kills palms permanently â€” coconut palm takes 6-8 years to start bearing. India loses an estimated â‚¹500-1,000 crore annually. Severe in Kerala, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Goa.

**Organic/Traditional Treatment:**
- **Pheromone traps:** Ferrugineol-based aggregation pheromone traps (bucket type) @ 1/hectare. Most effective mass-trapping tool â€” can trap 100+ adults/trap/month. Attract adults from 500m radius.
- Inject Metarhizium anisopliae (entomopathogenic fungus) suspension into trunk holes
- Apply Beauveria bassiana paste on wounds and cut surfaces
- Stem injection with neem oil (10%) through holes drilled into infested trunk
- Regular crown cleaning â€” remove dried leaves and fibre that harbour adult weevils

**Chemical Treatment:**
- **Trunk injection (curative):** Drill 2-3 holes (downward angle) into trunk near infestation zone. Inject Imidacloprid 17.8 SL (10 mL in 10 mL water) per hole OR Chlorpyrifos 20 EC (10 mL per hole). Seal holes with cement.
- **Crown treatment:** Pour Chlorpyrifos 20 EC @ 5 mL/L (1-2 L/palm) into leaf axils and crown area â€” preventive monthly treatment in endemic areas
- **Wound treatment:** Apply Carbaryl 50 WP paste (50 g + 1 L water) on all trunk wounds, cut surfaces, and leaf scars â€” prevents egg-laying
- **Soil drenching:** Imidacloprid 17.8 SL @ 3 mL/L â€” drench around palm base to kill emerging adults

**Prevention:**
- **NEVER leave open wounds on palm trunk** â€” dress all cuts immediately with Bordeaux paste or Carbaryl paste. RPW females lay eggs in wounds/cuts.
- Avoid cutting green leaves/fronds (creates wounds)
- Install pheromone traps year-round for early detection
- If palm is severely damaged (crown collapsed) â€” fell and burn immediately to prevent adults from emerging and spreading
- Fill leaf axils with sand + neem cake (1:1) as physical barrier
- Regular monitoring â€” inspect palms monthly for holes, frass, smell

### B8. Rhinoceros Beetle â€” Coconut, Oil Palm

**Scientific Name:** Oryctes rhinoceros (Linnaeus)
**Crop Affected:** Coconut (primary), Oil palm, Arecanut, Date palm
**Type:** Insect pest â€” Coleoptera (Scarabaeidae). Large black beetle (35-50 mm), horn on head (male). Adults bore into unopened leaves and crown.
**Season/Conditions:** Active year-round, peak during pre-monsoon (Apr-Jun) and post-monsoon (Oct-Nov). Adults active at night â€” fly from breeding sites (decaying organic matter) to palm crowns. **Symptoms:**
- **Leaf damage:** Characteristic "V-shaped" or triangular cuts on frond edges â€” looks like someone cut leaf with scissors. Young unopened leaves (spindle) show diamond/wedge-shaped holes when they unfurl.
- **Crown boring:** Adult beetle bores into base of spindle/unopened leaf. Bore hole (2-3 cm diameter) visible at crown centre with chewed fibre at entry. Beetle feeds on soft tissue inside.
- **Yield reduction:** Repeated damage to growing point reduces number of functional fronds â†’ reduced nut yield. Does not usually kill palm (unlike RPW) but weakens it.
- **Secondary damage:** Bore holes created by rhinoceros beetle serve as entry points for Red Palm Weevil (RPW) â€” this combination is lethal. Control rhinoceros beetle to prevent RPW invasion.

**Economic Impact:** Ubiquitous pest of coconut in India. Causes 10-15% yield reduction in endemic areas. More importantly, its bore holes invite RPW, making rhinoceros beetle control essential for preventing RPW attacks.

**Organic/Traditional Treatment:**
- **Metarhizium anisopliae (Green Muscardine Fungus):** Mix 5 kg M. anisopliae-colonized rice bran in breeding sites (manure/compost heaps) â€” infects and kills larvae. â­ MOST effective biocontrol.
- **Oryctes rhinoceros Nudivirus (OrNV):** Virus specific to rhinoceros beetle â€” release infected beetles (0.5 mL virus suspension fed to 30 adults, release in field) â€” spreads through population. Available from ICAR-CPCRI.
- Install rhinoceros beetle pheromone traps (Oryctalure) @ 5/ha â€” mass trapping of adults
- Leaf axil filling: Pack top 3 leaf axils with sand + naphthalene balls (2-3 per axil) or neem cake
- Hook out beetles: Check crown monthly â€” use hooked iron rod to extract beetles from bore holes

**Chemical Treatment:**
- **Crown treatment (preventive):** Pour Chlorpyrifos 20 EC @ 5 mL/L (1 L/palm) OR Carbaryl 50 WP 2% (20 g/L) into top 3 leaf axils at monthly intervals
- **Bore hole treatment:** Insert Aluminum Phosphide tablet (Celphos) 1/4 tablet into bore hole, seal with mud â€” fumigant kills beetle inside
- **Breeding site treatment:** Spray Chlorpyrifos 20 EC @ 5 mL/L on manure/compost heaps to kill larvae
- **Naphthalene balls:** Place 3-4 naphthalene balls in top 3 leaf axils at 45-day intervals â€” repels adults (widely practiced in Kerala and Karnataka)

**Prevention:**
- **Breeding site management (MOST important):** Do NOT leave decaying coconut logs, trunks, or organic debris unattended â€” these are prime breeding sites. Chop and burn dead coconut logs. Treat manure/compost heaps with Metarhizium before monsoon.
- Maintain clean plantation floor â€” remove fallen organic matter regularly
- If using FYM/compost, add Metarhizium or Beauveria formulation before application
- Cover compost pits with soil layer (30 cm) â€” prevents beetle oviposition
- Regular pheromone trapping from March (before peak flight)

### B9. Brinjal Shoot and Fruit Borer (BSFB)

**Scientific Name:** Leucinodes orbonalis (GuenÃ©e)
**Crop Affected:** Brinjal (eggplant/aubergine) â€” most destructive pest of brinjal in India and South Asia

- Healthy nursery practices â€” pest-free seedlings
- Clip and destroy infested shoots every week (cultural control reduces carryover)

### B10. Termites â€” Sugarcane, Wheat, Groundnut

**Scientific Name:** Odontotermes obesus (Indian mound-building termite), Microtermes obesi (subterranean termite)
**Crop Affected:** Sugarcane (most severe), Wheat, Groundnut, Cotton, Maize, Pulses, Trees, Orchards â€” nearly all crops in semi-arid and dry regions
**Type:** Insect pest â€” Isoptera (Termitidae). Social insects living in underground colonies. Damage roots and lower stem by feeding on cellulose.
**Season/Conditions:** Year-round, but damage peaks in dry periods when soil moisture is low (Oct-Feb in North India, Apr-Jun in South India). Colonies in soil â€” termitaria (mounds) visible in fields. Sandy and sandy-loam soils most affected. Organic matter-deficient soils have higher termite incidence.

**Symptoms:**
- **Sugarcane:** Setts (planting material) hollowed out â€” poor germination. Roots and lower stem gnawed â€” plant dries up. Galleries (mud tubes) visible on stem surface. Dead tillers in patches â€” pull plant and see hollow/eaten root system.
- **Wheat:** Patchy dead plants in field. Roots completely eaten â€” plant dries and turns white. Pull out â€” no roots, only muddy galleries. Damage often in pre-sowing/post-sowing dry period.
- **Groundnut:** Pods and taproot eaten. Pods have mud-filled cavities â€” no kernels. Above-ground plant may look healthy initially until root damage is severe.
- **Trees/Orchards:** Mud tubes running up trunk. Bark eaten from inside. Young trees killed â€” saplings particularly vulnerable.
- **General indicator:** Mud tubes/galleries (earthen tunnels) on soil surface, crop residues, or stem base â€” classic termite sign.

**Economic Impact:** Causes 10-30% cane loss in sugarcane, 20-40% yield loss in wheat (in endemic areas of Rajasthan, MP, Maharashtra), 15-25% pod damage in groundnut. Total annual loss estimated at â‚¹3,000-5,000 crore across crops. Particularly severe in dryland/rain-fed agriculture.

**Organic/Traditional Treatment:**
- Apply Beauveria bassiana @ 2.5 kg/ha mixed with FYM â€” broadcast before sowing and incorporate in soil
- Apply neem cake @ 250 kg/ha at sowing â€” antifeedant and repellent
- Flood irrigate before sowing (where possible) â€” drowns termite galleries
- Remove and destroy termite mounds (termitaria) in and around field â€” reduces colony source
- Apply castor cake or mustard cake @ 200 kg/ha â€” traditional practice, produces compounds toxic to termites
- Avoid burning crop residues â€” incorporate residues in soil with ploughing (provides alternative food, diverts termites from crop)

**Chemical Treatment:**
- **Seed/Sett treatment (preventive â€” most effective):** Treat sugarcane setts by dipping in Chlorpyrifos 20 EC @ 5 mL/L for 15 minutes before planting. For wheat: Chlorpyrifos 20 EC @ 4 mL/kg seed treatment.
- **Soil application:** Fipronil 0.3% GR @ 20-25 kg/ha â€” broadcast and incorporate in soil at sowing. â­ Most effective chemical for termites.
- **Alternative soil application:** Chlorpyrifos 20 EC @ 4 L/ha â€” mix with 50 kg sand, broadcast and irrigate
- **Standing crop treatment (emergency):** Drench Chlorpyrifos 20 EC @ 4 mL/L around affected plants â€” irrigate immediately after
- **Mound treatment:** Pour Chlorpyrifos 20 EC @ 10 mL/L into termite mound holes (200-500 mL per mound). Or inject Aluminum Phosphide (1-2 tablets) into mound and seal.
- **For trees:** Remove mud tubes, apply Chlorpyrifos paste (20 EC + soil) on trunk. Soil drench around root zone.

**Prevention:**
- Deep summer ploughing â€” exposes termite galleries and queen chamber to sun
- Maintain soil moisture through timely irrigation â€” termites prefer dry soil
- Apply FYM/compost regularly â€” improves soil biology, suppresses termites
- Avoid continuous wheat-wheat or wheat-fallow cropping in termite-endemic areas â€” rotate with crops less susceptible (rice in Kharif)
- Remove old tree stumps and woody residues from field â€” these are termite food sources
- Treated seed/sett is the single most cost-effective preventive measure

## SECTION D: Integrated Pest Management (IPM) Quick Reference

### IPM Principles for Indian Farming

> **What is IPM?** Integrated Pest Management is a science-based approach that combines multiple pest control methods to keep pest damage below economic injury levels while minimizing risks to human health and the environment. India's NCIPM (National Centre for Integrated Pest Management) promotes IPM as the national strategy for sustainable pest management.

1. **Cultural Control (Foundation â€” costs â‚¹0):**
   - **Crop rotation:** Rotate cereals with legumes (rice-chickpea, wheat-green gram). Breaks pest/disease cycles. Example: Rice-rice system promotes BPH/stem borer buildup; rice-pulse rotation breaks it.
   - **Resistant/tolerant varieties:** Use ICAR/SAU-recommended resistant varieties for your region. Examples: Samba Mahsuri (BPH-resistant rice), HD-3226 (rust-resistant wheat), Bt cotton (bollworm), RH-749 (aphid-tolerant mustard).
   - **Optimal sowing time:** Early sowing of mustard (before Oct 25) escapes aphid peak. Timely rice transplanting avoids stem borer peak.
   - **Clean cultivation:** Remove crop residues, weed hosts, volunteer plants. Destroy ratoon rice/stubble â€” harbours stem borer pupae.
   - **Proper spacing and nutrition:** Avoid excess nitrogen (attracts sucking pests). Maintain recommended spacing (avoids dense canopy favoured by BPH, blast).
   - **Trap cropping:** Marigold borders in tomato/brinjal (repels whitefly). Napier grass border around maize (traps FAW). Mustard border around cabbage (attracts diamondback moth away from crop).
   - **Deep summer ploughing:** Exposes soil-borne pests (pupae, grubs, termites) to sun, predators, and desiccation. Practice in April-May during fallow.

2. **Biological Control (Nature's army â€” low cost, high sustainability):**

3. **Mechanical/Physical Control (Labour-intensive but effective for smallholders):**
   - **Pheromone traps:** Species-specific lures â€” FAW, Helicoverpa, stem borer, fruit fly, pink bollworm, BSFB, rhinoceros beetle. Install @ 5-15/acre. Replace lures every 21-30 days.

   - **Yellow sticky traps:** Attract whitefly, aphids, leaf miners. Install @ 10-12/acre at canopy height. Replace every 7-10 days.
   - **Blue sticky traps:** Attract thrips. Use in onion, chilli fields.
   - **Hand-picking:** Collect and destroy egg masses (stem borer), infested fruits (brinjal borer), larvae (FAW from whorls). Effective in small plots.
   - **Clipping:** Clip rice seedling tips during transplanting (removes stem borer eggs). Clip infested brinjal shoots weekly.
   - **Bird perches:** T-shaped wooden perches @ 20/acre â€” attracts insectivorous birds (kingfisher, drongo, myna) that eat caterpillars. â­ MOST cost-effective and underutilised tool.
   - **Netting/bagging:** Insect-proof net houses for nurseries. Fruit bagging for mango (fruit fly protection). Net covers for vegetable beds.

4. **Chemical Control (Last resort â€” use only when ETL is crossed):**
   - **Economic Threshold Level (ETL):** Spray ONLY when pest population exceeds ETL â€” the pest density at which control cost equals the value of crop saved. Spraying below ETL wastes money and kills natural enemies.
   - **ETL examples:** BPH: 5-10/hill (tillering), 10-15/hill (flowering). Stem borer: >5% dead hearts or >2% white ears. Mustard aphid: >50 aphids/10 cm twig. FAW: >10% infested plants at whorl stage. Helicoverpa: >1 larva/m row in chickpea.

   - **Selective insecticides preferred:** Use target-specific chemicals that spare natural enemies. Avoid broad-spectrum pyrethroids and organophosphates as first choice.
   - **Safe practices:** Wear protective gear (mask, gloves, full clothing). Follow label dosage exactly â€” more â‰  better. Observe PHI (Pre-Harvest Interval) â€” do not harvest within PHI period. Dispose empty containers safely (triple rinse, puncture). Never spray near water bodies.
   - **CIB&RC registration:** Use ONLY CIB&RC (Central Insecticides Board) registered products. Check label for crop-wise approval.

### First Aid for Pesticide Poisoning

> **EMERGENCY:** Call **108 (Ambulance)** or **112 (Emergency)** immediately. Transport victim to nearest hospital.

**Symptoms of Pesticide Poisoning:**
- Organophosphate/Carbamate: Excessive sweating, salivation, tears, nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, pinpoint pupils, muscle twitching, difficulty breathing, confusion, seizures.
- Pyrethroid: Skin tingling/burning, dizziness, headache, nausea.
- Paraquat (banned but still found): Mouth/throat burns, nausea, then liver/lung failure within days. âš ï¸ NO ANTIDOTE â€” must reach hospital immediately.

**First Aid Steps:**
1. **REMOVE** victim from contaminated area to fresh air
2. **REMOVE** contaminated clothing, shoes, accessories
3. **If skin contact:** Wash thoroughly with soap and water (15+ minutes). Do NOT use kerosene/diesel to wash (traditional practice â€” harmful!)
4. **If eye contact:** Rinse eyes with clean water for 15+ minutes
5. **If swallowed:** Do NOT induce vomiting unless doctor instructs. Give water/milk to drink.
6. **If inhaled:** Move to fresh air, loosen clothing, keep warm
7. **KEEP the pesticide container/label** â€” take it to the hospital for identification
8. **Hospital treatment:** Atropine is the antidote for OP/Carbamate poisoning. Pralidoxime (2-PAM) for OPs. Activated charcoal for ingestion.

### Safe Pesticide Handling Rules

1. **READ THE LABEL** every time â€” even if you've used it before. Labels may update.
2. **Wear PPE (Personal Protective Equipment):** Full-sleeve shirt, long trousers, rubber gloves, face mask/respirator, goggles. At minimum: mask + gloves.
3. **Never eat, drink, or smoke while spraying.**
4. **Spray in early morning or late evening** â€” less wind drift, less heat exposure.
5. **Spray downwind** â€” never walk into your own spray cloud.
6. **Triple-rinse containers** after use, puncture/crush, and return to dealer or dispose at designated collection point. NEVER reuse pesticide containers for food/water storage.
7. **Store pesticides locked** â€” away from food, water, children, and animals. Store in original container with label.
8. **Follow dosage exactly** â€” "double dose for better result" is a myth. It wastes money, harms health, causes residues, and accelerates resistance.
9. **Maintain records** â€” date, pesticide, dose, target pest, weather. Useful for resistance management and traceability.
10. **Respect PHI** â€” do not harvest or sell produce until the Pre-Harvest Interval has passed.
